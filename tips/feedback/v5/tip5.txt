I studied the PDF and the LaTeX source structure. My blunt read: **the core idea is strong and distinctive, but the current paper feels like three documents merged into one**: a research paper, a standards specification, and an adoption/manual appendix. That is why it feels clunky and why the white space is so visible. The paper has a real thesis—**merge evidence is the new bottleneck for AI-assisted software work**—but the current layout buries that thesis under too many tables, float barriers, long checklists, raw registries, and manifesto-style sections. 

The biggest source-level layout culprits are visible in the raw source: the main `paper/jankurai.tex` is a long sequence of section inputs separated by repeated `\FloatBarrier`s, then switches into `\onecolumn` appendices before returning to `\twocolumn` for references. That is exactly the kind of structure that creates stranded tables, float-only pages, and large empty vertical gaps. ([GitHub][1]) The frontmatter also uses `IEEEtran`, `\raggedbottom`, a custom title page, and `\vfill`; that combination explains the first-page emptiness and the uneven bottoms across the paper. ([GitHub][2])

# Overall verdict

**This can become much stronger, but it needs a ruthless separation of “paper” from “standard.”**

Right now, the paper is rich but not tier-1 clean. A top-tier reader should be able to answer these five questions by page 3:

1. What is the problem?
2. What is the new abstraction?
3. What is the artifact or system?
4. What evidence shows it works?
5. What are the limits?

At the moment, the reader gets a powerful opening argument, then a long language-history framing, then many standards tables, then partial evaluation, then public repo scoring, then agent controls, UX, security, waivers, governance, language-choice scoring, reference architecture, bad-behavior matrices, related work, and appendices. Almost all of it is relevant to the Jankurai ecosystem, but not all of it belongs in the main research paper.

The tier-1 version should be much sharper:

**Main paper thesis:**
AI-assisted software changes fail at merge time because repositories lack machine-readable ownership, proof routing, evidence receipts, and witnessable merge decisions. Jankurai introduces continuous repository alignment and the merge witness as a repository-local conformance interface.

Everything else should serve that.

# The largest content problem: the paper’s center arrives too late

The strongest concept is **merge witness**. It is the paper’s most original and reviewable artifact. It appears in the abstract and then formally in Section IV, but the reader has already gone through a long “language chaos” introduction and multiple broad historical claims before the mechanism fully lands.

For a tier-1 paper, I would move the running checkout PR and merge-witness story much earlier. The current Section I starts with language history. That material is thoughtful, but it reads like a keynote essay. The better first move is the concrete AI pull-request failure:

> An agent changes checkout UI, a generated client, and an OpenAPI contract. CI is green. Reviewers ask: what evidence makes this safe to merge?

That example should open the paper. Then introduce Jankurai as the answer.

The language-history material should become either:

* a shortened motivation subsection near the end of the introduction, or
* a background appendix.

Right now, Section I is trying to prove too much before the paper has earned the reader’s trust.

# The largest layout problem: float control is fighting the paper

The white-space issue is not cosmetic only. It actively makes the paper feel less mature because pages look mechanically forced rather than designed.

The repeated `\FloatBarrier`s in the source are likely causing floats to be dumped before LaTeX has enough surrounding text to place them naturally. The `\raggedbottom` setting also permits uneven page bottoms instead of stretching vertical glue to align content. The custom title page with `\vfill` creates a half-empty first page. The `\onecolumn` appendix switch helps some large tables but also makes code/template pages feel like raw dumps rather than curated supplementary material. ([GitHub][1])

The fix is not “make every page dense.” The fix is to **make every page intentional**.

# Evaluation feedback

This is the most important content issue.

The current evaluation is honest but underpowered. It says the project has:

* a self-audit,
* a seed conformance suite,
* expected fixture decisions,
* public repo advisory scan,
* coverage mapping,
* detector maturity labels.

That is a good start. But a tier-1 reviewer will ask:

**Are the decisions actually observed?**
Right now, the paper says expected fixture decisions are not yet observed by a per-fixture runner. That is a blocker for strong empirical claims.

**Are findings calibrated?**
The public scan reports many hard findings. Reviewers will ask how many are true actionable issues versus missing Jankurai-specific artifacts.

**Does Jankurai improve outcomes?**
The paper currently proposes metrics but does not measure them.

**What is the overhead?**
How long does the audit run? How large are receipts? How much CI time is added?

**Can other implementations reproduce it?**
The paper needs a clear conformance-suite command and observed outputs.

## Minimal evaluation upgrade

Before submitting, add:

1. **Observed fixture results**
   For each fixture: expected decision, observed decision, primary rule, pass/fail.

2. **Public repo scan reproducibility**
   Repo, commit SHA, scan command, config, runtime, score, top finding families.

3. **Manual validation sample**
   Randomly sample maybe 100 findings. Label true actionable, acceptable tradeoff, false positive, Jankurai-missing-surface-only.

4. **Case study**
   One real PR or synthetic realistic PR where Jankurai blocks, repairs, then passes.

5. **Runtime overhead**
   Audit time, proof-lane time, artifact size.

6. **Ablation**
   Compare:

   * audit only,
   * audit + owner map,
   * audit + proof lanes,
   * full witness.

That would make the paper dramatically stronger.

# Layout fixes at the LaTeX level

Here are the highest-impact source changes.

## 1. Remove most `\FloatBarrier`s

Keep `\FloatBarrier` only:

* before appendices,
* before references,
* maybe after the evaluation section.

Do not put it after nearly every section. The current source sequence has many barriers, which encourages stranded floats. ([GitHub][1])

## 2. Replace `\raggedbottom` with `\flushbottom` after frontmatter

The frontmatter currently includes `\raggedbottom`. That makes bottom gaps more visible. ([GitHub][2])

Try:

```latex
\begin{document}
...
\end{titlepage}
\flushbottom
```

If `\flushbottom` creates bad vertical stretching, use it only for the main two-column paper and switch back to `\raggedbottom` for appendices.

## 3. Stop using a standalone title page for the conference version

For the conference-style paper:

```latex
\title{Jankurai: Continuous Repository Alignment for Evidence-Carrying Pull Requests}
\author{...}
\maketitle
```

Then place abstract normally.

For the branded standard PDF, keep the cover page. But do not use the branded cover for the research submission.

## 4. Use `table*` deliberately for wide tables

Some tables are too wide for one column but too small to justify a page. Use `table*` at the top of pages for important wide tables only:

```latex
\begin{table*}[t]
\centering
\caption{...}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{...}
...
\end{tabularx}
\end{table*}
```

But do not overuse `table*`, because it can only float to top/bottom in many formats.

## 5. Move raw registries to appendix or supplement

Full HLT registries, public repo lists, YAML/TOML templates, and language matrices should not compete with the paper argument.

## 6. Use compact table macros

For dense tables:

```latex
\scriptsize
\setlength{\tabcolsep}{3pt}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.05}
```

But use this sparingly. If a table requires `\scriptsize` and still looks cramped, it probably belongs in the appendix.

## 7. Balance references

Before bibliography:

```latex
\IEEEtriggeratref{55}
```

or use an accepted balancing method for your target venue. This is secondary but helps the final page.

# Suggested new abstract

Here is a tighter version:

> AI-assisted coding lowers the cost of plausible patches but does not lower the cost of proving that a patch is safe to merge. In large repositories, the missing evidence is often not syntax-level correctness but repository-local alignment: which owner is responsible for the changed path, which generated boundaries apply, which proof lane is required, which evidence is fresh, and which waiver or repair makes a deviation acceptable. This paper introduces Jankurai, a stack-neutral conformance model for continuous repository alignment. Jankurai’s central artifact is the merge witness: a versioned, repository-local binding among changed paths, owner routes, generated-zone status, required proof receipts, observed evidence, artifact digests, tool identity, commit identity, missing evidence, and a closed merge decision. We define the core artifact model, conformance levels, receipt freshness rules, repair queues, and waiver semantics; describe an early reference auditor; and report seed conformance and advisory public-repository scan evidence. Jankurai does not prove full program semantics and does not prescribe a language stack. Its claim is narrower: AI- and human-authored pull requests should merge only when the repository can emit current, auditable evidence for the change.

This keeps the soul but reads more like a top-tier paper.

3. Repository boundaries are currently implicit.
4. Jankurai makes them explicit.
5. The merge witness binds change, owner, proof, evidence, and decision.
6. Missing evidence becomes a repair queue, not reviewer intuition.

That is the paper.

# Final blunt assessment

The paper has a genuinely exciting core. **“Merge witness” is strong. “Repository as alignment layer” is strong. “Evidence debt” is strong.** The problem is that the current manuscript tries to ship the whole Jankurai universe at once.

For a tier-1 paper, make the main PDF narrower, cleaner, more empirical, and more visual. Put the standard machinery in appendices. Kill the float barriers. Stop letting small tables own whole pages. Replace registry density with diagrams and observed results.

The target feeling should be:

> “This paper proposes a new repository-local merge evidence object, defines its semantics, implements an auditor, and shows that current repositories lack this interface.”

That is crisp, defensible, and much more likely to read like a serious top-tier systems/software-engineering paper.
