I studied the PDF and the source layout. The paper has a strong core idea: **repository-local merge evidence for AI-assisted code**, centered on **owner maps, proof lanes, receipts, repair queues, waivers, and a merge witness**. That is genuinely promising. The biggest problem is that the current paper reads less like a tight tier-1 research paper and more like a standards manifesto plus implementation notes plus stack philosophy plus appendix material. The white space and “clunky” feel are not cosmetic accidents; they come from the paper’s current structure, float strategy, table density, and LaTeX source choices. 

## Executive diagnosis

The paper should become **one sharper paper**, not a larger paper. Right now it is trying to be all of these at once:

1. A research paper on continuous repository alignment.
2. A standard specification.
3. A tool paper for the `jankurai` CLI.
4. A taxonomy paper for “vibe artifacts.”
5. A public-repository scoring report.
6. A non-normative stack-ranking essay.
7. A practical agent-repository handbook.
8. A UX/security/toolchain policy guide.

That breadth is exciting, but it is also the reason it feels clunky. A tier-1 paper needs a narrower spine:

**Problem → model → artifact → implementation → evaluation → limitations.**

The strongest tier-1 framing is:

> **Jankurai introduces continuous repository alignment: a repository-local conformance model that binds changed paths, owners, proof lanes, evidence receipts, missing evidence, waivers, and repair actions into a reproducible merge witness for human- and agent-authored pull requests.**

Everything that does not directly support that sentence should be shortened, moved to appendix, or spun out.

# 1. The biggest content issue: the contribution is strong, but buried

The abstract has the right claim: Jankurai treats the repository as the alignment layer; the merge witness binds changed paths, owner routes, proof receipts, observed evidence, score deltas, missing evidence, artifact digests, tool identity, and commit identity. That is the paper. The first pages then wander through programming-language history, technical debt, Julia, TypeScript, Brooks, Dijkstra, Conway, and AI coding reports before the reader gets a crisp technical object.

For a tier-1 venue, the paper should expose the central object much earlier.

## Current problem

The introduction spends too much time proving that software history is messy. That story is interesting, but it delays the research contribution. The current Section I is well-written in places, but it feels like a keynote essay rather than the opening of a top conference paper.

The reader should understand by the end of page 1:

* What problem exists.
* Why existing CI/CODEOWNERS/Scorecard/SLSA/OpenAPI/testing are insufficient alone.
* What Jankurai adds.
* What artifact is emitted.
* What evidence you evaluated.

Right now, the title page plus abstract consumes the entire first PDF page, and the introduction begins on PDF page 2. The actual technical definition of the merge witness does not become visual until PDF page 7. That is too late.

## Recommended rewrite

Make the first two pages follow this structure:

**Paragraph 1 — Problem.**
AI-assisted coding makes plausible patches cheap, but merge safety depends on repository-local evidence that is often missing: path ownership, proof routing, generated-source provenance, security evidence, UX evidence, and waiver status.

**Paragraph 2 — Gap.**
Existing tools produce fragments: tests, CI logs, CODEOWNERS review, SCA/SAST results, screenshots, schemas, provenance. They do not bind those fragments into a changed-path-specific merge decision.

**Paragraph 3 — Contribution.**
Jankurai defines continuous repository alignment and the merge witness.

**Paragraph 4 — Running example.**
Checkout PR touches UI, generated client, and OpenAPI contract. Show how the witness blocks until required receipts exist.

**Paragraph 5 — Evaluation.**
State exactly what is implemented and measured. Do not hide that the current seed suite is inventory-only; either finish the per-fixture runner or make this a standards/design paper.

**Contributions list.**
Keep it short, concrete, and numbered.

Suggested contribution list:

> This paper contributes:
>
> 1. A repository-local conformance model for AI-assisted pull requests, mapping changed paths to owner routes, generated zones, proof lanes, receipts, waivers, and repair actions.
> 2. The merge witness, a schema-valid artifact that binds changed paths, required evidence, observed evidence, artifact digests, tool identity, commit identity, and a closed merge decision.
> 3. A stable rule registry for vibe artifacts, with repair metadata and proof-lane routing.
> 4. A reference implementation and seed conformance suite, including current limitations and the next empirical evaluation plan.
> 5. An advisory public-repository scan showing that conventional CI maturity does not automatically expose merge-witness-ready evidence.

That is enough. Do not add stack ranking as a main contribution.

# 2. The paper’s best parts

These are the parts I would protect and strengthen.

## 2.1 Merge witness is the core intellectual artifact

The merge witness is the strongest idea in the paper. Figure 2 and the surrounding text define a concrete artifact that binds changed paths, owners, required receipts, present receipts, missing evidence, artifact digests, score delta, and decision. This is the part that feels novel and useful.

But Figure 2 is currently buried on PDF page 7. Move a compact version to page 2 or page 3. The reader should see the witness before seeing long tables of levels and terms.

## 2.2 “Score is posture. Witness is decision. Conformance is pass/fail.”

This is excellent. Keep it. It is one of the clearest lines in the paper.

It also protects the work from a common reviewer attack: “Is this just another score?” The answer is no. The score is advisory posture; the witness is a merge decision; conformance is binary for a claimed level.

Make this a boxed principle early in the standard section.

## 2.3 The running checkout PR works

The checkout-flow example is a good anchor: UI change, generated client, OpenAPI contract, missing UX evidence, missing regeneration proof, blocked witness, repair queue. Keep this example and reuse it throughout the paper.

But it should do more work. Right now it appears early and then the paper returns to large tables. Instead, use the same checkout PR in:

* Introduction.
* Definitions.
* Merge witness.
* Proof freshness.
* Repair queue.
* Evaluation fixture.

That will make the paper feel coherent instead of encyclopedic.

## 2.4 The paper is admirably honest about limitations

The evaluation section states that Jankurai is currently a standards proposal with an early reference implementation, not a completed empirical benchmark. It also says the seed conformance command validates fixture inventory and expected JSON presence but does not yet emit observed per-fixture witness decisions. That honesty is good, but for a tier-1 paper it is also dangerous unless the paper is framed as a systems/design/conformance paper rather than an empirical results paper. The source section makes this limitation explicit. ([GitHub][1])

My recommendation: finish the observed fixture runner before submission. The paper will become much stronger immediately.

# 3. The biggest content risks for a tier-1 venue

## 3.1 The paper overclaims “standard” before conformance evidence is mature

Calling this a “standard” is bold. That can work, but tier-1 reviewers will ask:

* Who has implemented it besides the reference implementation?
* What is the conformance suite?
* What artifacts are mandatory?
* Can another implementation pass?
* How many repositories has it been tested on?
* What are false positive and false negative rates?
* What is the runtime cost?
* What is the developer burden?

The paper partially answers some of these, but not enough. The current seed suite is expected-output/inventory-oriented rather than observed-decision-oriented. That makes the “standard” claim feel aspirational.

### Fix

Use more precise language:

* In the title/abstract: “standard proposal” or “conformance model.”
* In the evaluation: “reference implementation evidence.”
* In the conclusion: “candidate standard interface.”

Then, once the conformance suite emits actual observed decisions, upgrade the claim.

## 3.2 The public repository scoring section is useful but risky

The advisory scan of 30 public repositories is interesting and potentially powerful. The paper reports that all 30 scanned repositories were below a 50-point red band, with a maximum score of 48 and an average score of 33.4. In the paper, this is carefully caveated as an advisory posture scan rather than certification. That caveat is good.

Still, this section can trigger reviewer resistance. The table names prominent projects and assigns low scores. Even though the paper says these are evidence gaps rather than quality accusations, the presentation still looks like a leaderboard of public failure.

### Fix

In the main paper, replace the named top-10 ranking with an anonymized or aggregate result:

* “30 public repositories scanned.”
* “Common missing surfaces.”
* “Weak-dimension frequency.”
* “Examples of evidence gaps.”
* “Manual validation sample.”
* “False-positive analysis.”

Move the named full table to appendix or supplemental material. The GitHub source already has a dedicated public-repo scoring appendix in the paper build, so this is a natural move. The main source includes `08_public_repo_scoring` and an appendix file for public scores, so the structure is already close. ([GitHub][2])

## 3.3 The stack-ranking/reference-profile material should not be in the main paper

Sections XIV–XVIII are the most dangerous content for tier-1 review. They discuss languages as proof-cost compression, standard gravity, non-normative reference profile scoring, reference profile comparison, and a Rust/TypeScript/PostgreSQL architecture profile.

Even though you repeatedly say the stack score is non-normative, its presence in the main paper changes the perceived thesis. Reviewers may think:

* “Is this a Jankurai paper or a Rust/TS/Postgres advocacy paper?”
* “Why are Go/.NET/JVM/Rails/Python/Elixir being scored in a conformance paper?”
* “Are the weights validated?”
* “Is this necessary for the merge witness contribution?”

For a tier-1 paper, remove most of this from the main body.

### Keep only this in the main paper

A short paragraph:

> Jankurai Core is stack-neutral. The reference implementation uses a Rust/TypeScript/PostgreSQL profile, but conformance depends only on emitted artifacts: owner routes, proof receipts, generated-zone evidence, security evidence, UX evidence where applicable, waivers, repair queues, and merge witnesses.

Move the rest to appendix, blog, whitepaper, or companion standard.

## 3.4 Too many rule tables blur the actual model

The paper has many tables: definitions, threat model, artifact model, checklist, conformance levels, control-plane surfaces, taxonomy, toolchain behaviors, proof lanes, freshness algorithm, UX detectors, security obligations, permissions, waivers, governance, schema compatibility, score bands, ownership cells, rule metadata, repair metadata, coverage summary, public repo scoring, language bad-behavior matrix.

Individually, many are useful. Collectively, they exhaust the reader.

### Fix

Use three levels:

**Main paper:** only the tables required to understand the model.

Keep:

* Core terms, shortened.
* Threat model, shortened.
* Core artifact model.
* Merge-witness fields.
* Conformance levels.
* Evaluation results.
* One compact rule-family table.
* One related-work boundary table.

**Appendix:** full registries and matrices.

Move:

* Full vibe taxonomy.
* Toolchain bad-behavior matrix.
* Permission matrix.
* Security obligations by level.
* Governance gates.
* Schema compatibility.
* Reference profile scoring.
* Ownership cells.
* Public repo full ranking.
* Repair template details.

**Supplemental repository artifacts:** machine-readable registry, full public scan data, fixture expected JSON, receipts.

# 4. Evaluation feedback: this is the main barrier to “tier-1”

The evaluation section is the biggest difference between a strong standard proposal and a tier-1 paper.

## 4.1 Current evaluation claim is too weak

The paper says the seed suite has 10 fixture directories and 12 expected JSON files, with one pass fixture and nine block fixtures. It also states that the current command validates fixture inventory and expected JSON presence but does not yet emit observed per-fixture witness decisions. ([GitHub][1])

That means the evaluation currently demonstrates:

* Artifact presence.
* Expected outcome definitions.
* Self-audit outputs.
* A public advisory scan.

It does not yet demonstrate:

* Actual per-fixture observed decisions.
* Detection accuracy.
* False positives.
* False negatives.
* Repair success.
* Time-to-repair.
* Review-time reduction.
* Agent-context reduction.
* Conformance portability across implementations.

## 4.2 Minimum evaluation needed before serious submission

Add these experiments.

### Experiment 1 — Conformance fixture execution

For every fixture:

* Run `jankurai witness`.
* Emit observed witness JSON.
* Compare observed decision to expected decision.
* Check expected rule ID.
* Check missing evidence list.
* Check repair queue fields.
* Check artifact digests.

Report:

| Metric                               |         Needed |
| ------------------------------------ | -------------: |
| Fixtures                             | at least 20–40 |
| Positive pass fixtures               |     at least 5 |
| Negative block fixtures              |    at least 15 |
| Expected/observed decision agreement |            yes |
| Expected/observed rule agreement     |            yes |
| Witness schema validation            |            yes |
| Repair queue schema validation       |            yes |

Right now, the paper’s own limitations say this is future work. Make it current work.

### Experiment 2 — Ablation over evidence surfaces

Show what happens when you remove one control at a time:

* Owner map absent.
* Test map absent.
* Generated-zones manifest absent.
* Receipt stale.
* Receipt wrong commit.
* Receipt missing digest.
* UX proof absent.
* Secret scan absent.
* Waiver expired.

The paper’s proof freshness algorithm is good; now demonstrate it.

### Experiment 3 — Public-repo scan with manual validation

The public scan is compelling but needs calibration.

For a sample of findings:

* Randomly sample 100 findings across 30 repos.
* Manually classify true positive / false positive / unclear.
* Report precision by rule family.
* Do not claim recall unless you have ground truth.
* Separate “Jankurai adoption missing” from “engineering risk present.”

This will make the public scan much harder to dismiss.

### Experiment 4 — Case study on one real PR

Use the checkout PR or a real historical PR.

Show:

1. Changed paths.
2. Owner routing.
3. Required lanes.
4. Present receipts.
5. Missing evidence.
6. Initial decision.
7. Repair queue.
8. Repair actions.
9. Final witness.
10. Human review implications.

This is more valuable than five more tables.

### Experiment 5 — Runtime and burden

Tier-1 reviewers will ask about overhead.

Report:

* Time for audit.
* Time for witness generation.
* Time for proof lane selection.
* CI runtime impact.
* Number of files required to adopt HL1/HL2/HL3.
* Number of lines of policy added.
* False-positive repair burden.

### Experiment 6 — Agent efficiency, if claimed

The paper repeatedly says this improves agent efficiency. That claim is plausible, but it needs direct evidence.

Measure:

* Tokens in full-repo context vs Jankurai context pack.
* Proof lane selection accuracy.
* First-pass repair success.
* Time-to-fix for agent with and without owner/test maps.
* Number of irrelevant files read.

Without that, keep “agent efficiency” as a hypothesis or future metric.

# 5. Layout diagnosis: why the paper feels clunky

The layout issues have identifiable causes in the source.

The source uses `IEEEtran`, `\raggedbottom`, `placeins`, custom `fancyhdr`, a separate `titlepage`, a large header graphic, and a custom abstract block. The frontmatter source also uses `\vfill` inside the title page, which explains the huge blank lower area on PDF page 1. ([GitHub][3])

The main wrapper uses many `\FloatBarrier` commands between section inputs, then switches to `\onecolumn` for appendices and later back to `\twocolumn` before references. That combination is a classic cause of underfilled pages, float-only pages, and forced blank space. The GitHub source shows the section-input wrapper and the repeated barriers/mode switches. ([GitHub][4])

## 5.1 Measured white-space hotspots

The worst visible white-space pages are:

| PDF page | Symptom                                          | Likely cause                                                    | Fix                                                                                  |
| -------: | ------------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
|        1 | Bottom third mostly blank                        | Separate `titlepage` plus `\vfill`; abstract block is narrow    | Remove title page; use normal IEEE first page or `\twocolumn[...]`; shorten abstract |
|        4 | Large blank lower half                           | Early float/section barrier around Figure 1 and running example | Remove nearby `\FloatBarrier`; move Table II closer; reduce flow figure              |
|     9–10 | Figure 3 appears isolated with large white space | `figure*` plus barrier/float placement                          | Replace pie with compact one-column bar chart or inline table; avoid `figure*`       |
|       12 | Underfilled page around full-width table         | Wide table float placement                                      | Move Table XIII to appendix or combine with Appendix G                               |
|       25 | Almost empty page with one small table           | Non-normative score-band table isolated                         | Move Table XLIV to appendix or combine with surrounding reference-profile material   |
|       26 | Table begins low, page mostly empty              | Float placement after previous isolated table                   | Move Table XLV to appendix; avoid forced float sequencing                            |
|       35 | Large blank lower half before references         | Appendix table followed by `\clearpage \twocolumn`              | Remove forced clearpage before references or keep appendix/reference mode consistent |

## 5.2 The main LaTeX culprits

### Culprit 1 — Too many `\FloatBarrier`s

The paper wrapper inserts barriers after major inputs. This prevents floats from naturally moving to good positions. It protects local ordering, but it also forces LaTeX to dump floats early and leave pages underfilled. ([GitHub][4])

**Fix:**
Use barriers only before appendices and before a major section where float leakage would be confusing.

Suggested rule:

```tex
% Avoid barriers after every section.
% Keep only:
\FloatBarrier % before Evaluation, if necessary
\FloatBarrier % before Appendices
```

Better: remove all early barriers, compile, inspect, then add back only the ones that fix real float drift.

### Culprit 2 — `\raggedbottom`

`\raggedbottom` makes pages end unevenly instead of stretching vertical glue. It can be pleasant for drafts but creates exactly the “white area” look you dislike. It is present in the frontmatter source. ([GitHub][3])

**Fix:**
Use default `\flushbottom` for the main two-column paper. If appendices need flexibility, switch only there.

```tex
% Main paper
\flushbottom

% Optional: only in appendix if needed
\appendices
\raggedbottom
```

### Culprit 3 — Separate title page

A tier-1 conference paper should not spend page 1 as a decorative title page unless the venue requires it. PDF page 1 has a large logo/header, title, abstract, and then a large blank bottom area. The frontmatter source creates a `titlepage` and uses `\vfill`. ([GitHub][3])

**Fix:**
Use a compact first-page header and begin the introduction on page 1.

For example:

```tex
\begin{document}
\twocolumn[
  \begin{@twocolumnfalse}
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.22\textwidth]{...}
  {\Large\bfseries Jankurai: Continuous Repository Alignment ...\par}
  \vspace{0.4em}
  \begin{abstract}
  ...
  \end{abstract}
  \vspace{0.8em}
  \end{@twocolumnfalse}
]
\section{Introduction}
```

Or remove the logo completely for a submission build.

### Culprit 4 — Too many `table*` and `figure*` floats

Wide two-column floats can only be placed in limited positions. When there are many of them, they pile up and create float-only or underfilled pages. The taxonomy source uses full-width `table*` and `figure*` for Table XII and Figure 3. ([GitHub][5])

**Fix:**
Use fewer full-width floats. Prefer:

* One-column table for compact summaries.
* Appendix for full registry.
* Small inline diagrams.
* `table*` only for essential, genuinely wide data.

### Culprit 5 — One-column appendices plus mode switching

The source switches to `\onecolumn` for appendices, then later `\clearpage \twocolumn` for references. That explains the appendix/reference transition feeling clunky. ([GitHub][4])

**Fix options:**

1. Keep appendices two-column and use `table*` only for the few huge tables.
2. Put references immediately after conclusion, then appendices after references if the venue allows.
3. Keep appendices one-column but do not force a blank transition page before references.
4. Move large appendices to supplemental material.

# 7. Source-tree feedback

The GitHub paper directory contains `figures`, `sections`, `tex`, `citation-index.md`, `jankurai.md`, `jankurai.pdf`, `jankurai.tex`, `outline.md`, and bibliography files. ([GitHub][6]) The main wrapper actually inputs files from `paper/tex/sections` and `paper/tex/appendices`, not the top-level `paper/sections` Markdown directory. ([GitHub][4])

That is a potential source-of-truth problem.

## Fix

Add a `paper/README.md` saying:

```text
Canonical source:
  paper/jankurai.tex
  paper/tex/frontmatter.tex
  paper/tex/sections/*.tex
  paper/tex/appendices/*.tex
  paper/references.bib

Generated artifacts:
  paper/jankurai.pdf
  paper/jankurai.md

Legacy or planning material:
  paper/sections/*.md
  paper/outline.md
```

Or move old Markdown sections under `paper/archive/`.

This matters because the paper itself argues against adapter drift and generated/source ambiguity. The paper repo should model that discipline.

# 8. Concrete LaTeX cleanup plan

Here is the order I would apply changes.

## Step 1 — Remove the title page

Replace:

```tex
\begin{titlepage}
...
\vfill
\end{titlepage}
```

with a compact IEEE-style first page.

This alone will remove the worst white area and make the paper feel more serious.

## Step 2 — Remove most `\FloatBarrier`s

The wrapper currently uses barriers repeatedly between section inputs and before the appendix/references transition. ([GitHub][4]) Start by keeping only one before appendices.

## Step 3 — Stop using global `\raggedbottom`

Change to:

```tex
\flushbottom
```

Use `\raggedbottom` only in appendices if necessary.

## Step 4 — Reduce full-width floats

Audit every `table*` and `figure*`.

Keep full-width only for:

* Merge witness anatomy.
* Evaluation results.
* Maybe related-work comparison.

Move full registries to appendix.

## Step 5 — Tighten float spacing

Add conservative spacing:

```tex
\setlength{\textfloatsep}{6pt plus 2pt minus 2pt}
\setlength{\floatsep}{6pt plus 2pt minus 2pt}
\setlength{\intextsep}{6pt plus 2pt minus 2pt}
\setlength{\abovecaptionskip}{3pt}
\setlength{\belowcaptionskip}{2pt}
```

Be careful if submitting to a strict IEEE venue; some venues dislike aggressive formatting. But for your own paper build, this will help.

## Step 6 — Use per-table density, not global density

The source globally sets `\arraystretch` to 1.13. That makes big tables taller. ([GitHub][3]) Use normal spacing globally and increase only for small tables.

```tex
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.0}
```

Then for readable small tables:

```tex
{\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.08}
\begin{tabularx}...
}
```

## Step 7 — Move appendices back to two-column unless needed

Do not globally switch all appendices to one-column. Use one-column only for specific huge artifacts.

## Step 8 — Remove `\clearpage` before references if possible

The source clears the page and switches back to two-column before references. ([GitHub][4]) That contributes to the blank lower half before references.

# 13. Specific content additions that would make it much stronger

## 13.1 Formalize the witness decision function

Add a compact formal definition:

```text
decision(R, C, Δ) =
  pass           if all required predicates satisfied
  review         if only review-routed evidence is missing/ambiguous
  block          if required evidence is absent or invalid
  ratchet_fail   if posture regresses without active waiver
  release_fail   if release-level predicates fail
```

Then define predicates:

* `owned(path)`
* `routed(path)`
* `generated_ok(path)`
* `receipt_fresh(lane, commit, paths)`
* `security_ok(change)`
* `waiver_valid(finding, commit, time)`
* `repair_queue_complete(finding)`

This would make the model feel rigorous.

## 13.2 Add a witness validity algorithm

Instead of only prose/table, show algorithmic steps:

```text
Algorithm 1: BuildMergeWitness
Input: repo R, base commit B, head commit H
1. Δ ← changed_paths(B, H)
2. owners ← route_owner(Δ, owner-map)
3. lanes ← route_proof(Δ, test-map)
4. zones ← check_generated_zones(Δ)
5. receipts ← load_receipts(lanes)
6. valid ← verify_freshness(receipts, H, Δ, maps)
7. findings ← detect_missing_evidence(...)
8. decision ← decide(findings, waivers, level)
9. emit witness with digests
```

This would replace multiple tables and improve readability.

## 13.3 Add one real artifact excerpt from the current repo

Use an actual self-audit/witness artifact, not only illustrative JSON. The current Figure 2 is compact and useful, but reviewers will ask what is real vs example.

Add labels:

* “Illustrative excerpt”
* “Observed artifact from self-audit”
* “Expected fixture output”

Do not blur those.

## 13.4 Add a reproducibility box

Example:

```text
Artifact availability:
Repository: ...
Commit: ...
Standard version: 0.7.0
Auditor version: 0.7.0
Schema version: 1.5.0
Commands:
  just conformance
  cargo run -p jankurai -- audit ...

Outputs:
  agent/repo-score.json
  target/jankurai/merge-witness.json
  ...
```

The source already emphasizes versioned artifacts and manifests; make that easy for the reader.

# 18. Bottom-line judgment

This paper has a real idea. The phrase I would build around is:

> **The repository is the alignment layer.**

The merge witness is the contribution. The rule registry and repair queue make it practical. The evaluation needs to catch up. The layout needs to stop fighting the reader.

The fastest path to a much stronger version is:

1. **Make it a merge-witness paper, not a general anti-vibe manifesto.**
2. **Move non-normative stack and language material to appendices.**
3. **Turn the evaluation from expected/inventory evidence into observed conformance evidence.**
4. **Replace float-heavy table sprawl with a few high-signal figures and compact tables.**
5. **Fix the LaTeX float barriers, title page, `\raggedbottom`, and appendix mode switches.**

Do those, and the paper will feel dramatically more serious, cleaner, and closer to a top-tier systems/software-engineering submission.
