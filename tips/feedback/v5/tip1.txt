I reviewed the uploaded 37-page rendered PDF and the raw TeX entry points/source structure. The strongest issue is **not just “too much white area.”** The deeper problem is that the paper is trying to be **a research paper, a standard, a tool manual, a taxonomy registry, a public scoring report, a stack-position paper, and an appendix-heavy specification all at once**. That is why the layout feels clunky: the LaTeX float system is being forced to carry too many wide tables and code blocks, while the narrative is repeatedly interrupted by standard-style checklists. The raw `jankurai.tex` source also confirms the layout cause: repeated `\FloatBarrier` calls, an appendix `\clearpage`, a `\onecolumn` appendix block, and a later `\twocolumn` switch are all visible in the source wrapper. Those choices almost guarantee float-only pages and awkward empty zones when combined with many `table*`/wide tables. ([GitHub][1]) 

## Executive verdict

This has a real paper inside it. The core idea is excellent: **repository-local merge evidence for AI/human pull requests**, centered on a **merge witness** that binds changed paths, owners, proof receipts, missing evidence, artifact digests, tool identity, and commit identity. That is memorable, useful, and timely.

But in its current form, it does **not yet read like a tier-1 paper**. It reads like a strong standards whitepaper with a lot of ambitious material. The current draft is too broad, too table-heavy, too float-fragmented, and too cautious in the evaluation section to support its strongest claims. The paper repeatedly tells the reader that Jankurai is important, but it does not yet give the clean empirical spine that a top venue would expect.

The fastest path upward is:

1. **Make “merge witness” the center of the paper.**
2. **Move most normative checklist material to appendices or a companion standard.**
3. **Replace the language/stack ranking material with either a short discussion or a separate profile document.**
4. **Turn the evaluation from “expected fixture shape + advisory scan” into observed, reproducible results.**
5. **Remove the source-level layout traps: title page, aggressive float barriers, one-column appendix switch, float-only tables, and oversized code blocks.**

## The main layout problem: the white space is structural, not cosmetic

The biggest white areas are not caused by margins being too large. They are caused by the document flow. In the source, `jankurai.tex` inputs sections, then repeatedly inserts `\FloatBarrier`; later it forces a `\clearpage`, switches appendices to `\onecolumn`, then clears again and switches back to `\twocolumn` for references. That pattern is especially dangerous in IEEE two-column layouts because wide floats often cannot appear where the author expects, so LaTeX defers them, flushes them, or isolates them on pages with very little text. ([GitHub][1])

The frontmatter source also defines a custom title page, custom table coloring, `fancyhdr` footer styling, `\raggedbottom`, and a large amount of macro setup in one dense file. That makes the rendered paper look more like a branded draft/specification than an archival conference paper. ([GitHub][2])

The visible result is:

* **Page 1 / cover:** huge lower-half white area.
* **Rendered page 3 / footer p. 2:** right column ends early, leaving a large blank area.
* **Rendered page 4 / footer p. 3:** bottom half is mostly empty after the running example and definitions start.
* **Rendered page 10 / footer p. 9:** the TLR pie-chart figure sits in the middle of a nearly empty page.
* **Rendered page 12 / footer p. 11:** one table floats alone in the vertical center of the page.
* **Rendered pages 25-26 / footer pp. 24-25:** small reference-profile tables are isolated on mostly blank pages.
* **Appendix pages 31-33:** large boxed code/file-tree blocks create a technical-manual feel rather than a polished research-paper feel. 

The cure is not to “tighten margins.” The cure is to reduce float count, remove unnecessary float barriers, keep appendices in a separate supplement, and convert many tables into prose, compact figures, or appendix-only artifacts.

## The paper’s strongest core

The strongest contribution is this sentence-level idea:

> A pull request should not be accepted because it looks plausible; it should be accepted because the repository can produce current, local, replayable evidence for the changed paths.

That is the paper. Everything should serve that.

The strongest named concepts are:

* **Vibe artifact:** plausible but unauditable repository residue.
* **Evidence debt:** technical debt expressed as missing/stale proof.
* **Continuous repository alignment:** keeping owners, lanes, generated boundaries, receipts, waivers, and repair queues synchronized.
* **Merge witness:** the central artifact that binds the decision.
* **Repair queue:** the action surface for agents and humans.
* **Agent-first repository design:** repository structure that makes correct agent behavior easier than guesswork.

The paper should make these six terms unavoidable and remove anything that distracts from them.

## The biggest content issue: it is several papers glued together

Right now the paper contains at least six separable documents:

1. **Research paper:** “Continuous Repository Alignment for Proof-Carrying Pull Requests.”
2. **Standard/specification:** conformance levels, MUST/SHOULD, stable rule IDs, schema fields.
3. **Tool paper:** Jankurai CLI, audit reports, witness command, proof lanes.
4. **Taxonomy paper:** vibe-artifact taxonomy and RPN/TLR model.
5. **Empirical/advisory scan paper:** 30 public repository scan.
6. **Architecture/profile essay:** Rust/TypeScript/PostgreSQL scoring, language history, stack gravity, bad behavior matrices.

A tier-1 paper needs one dominant spine. The right spine is:

**Problem -> model -> system design -> implementation -> evaluation -> limitations.**

The standards material should support the model. The taxonomy should support the evaluation. The public scan should be one result, not a major rhetorical pillar. The language/profile material should be largely removed from the main paper.

## Recommended new title and positioning

The current title, **“Anti-Vibe Coding Standard,”** is memorable, but it risks sounding like a manifesto. For a tier-1 submission, I would lead with the serious technical contribution and keep “vibe artifacts” as the internal term.

Better title options:

**Best serious title:**

> Continuous Repository Alignment for Proof-Carrying Pull Requests

**With system name:**

> Jankurai: Continuous Repository Alignment for Proof-Carrying Pull Requests

**If you want to preserve the hook:**

> Jankurai: Detecting and Repairing Vibe Artifacts with Merge Witnesses

I would avoid making “Anti-Vibe Coding Standard” the main archival title. It can be the subtitle, project tagline, or companion-standard title.

## Abstract feedback

The current abstract is too long and list-heavy. It tries to define the whole standard in the abstract. It also repeats concepts: repository alignment, proof receipts, merge witness, repair queues, stack-neutral model, bounded claim. Good ideas, but the density is high.

A tier-1 abstract should have five beats:

1. AI-assisted development makes plausible edits cheap.
2. The missing object is merge evidence tied to changed paths.
3. Jankurai defines a repository-local merge witness and conformance model.
4. Implementation/evaluation summary with concrete numbers.
5. Bounded conclusion.

A cleaner abstract shape:

> AI-assisted coding lowers the cost of plausible edits but does not make repository intent, ownership, generated boundaries, or proof obligations explicit. We introduce Jankurai, a repository-local conformance model for proof-carrying pull requests. Jankurai maps changed paths to owners, generated-zone policy, proof lanes, evidence receipts, waivers, and repair actions, then binds the result into a merge witness: a versioned artifact containing changed paths, required and observed evidence, artifact digests, tool identity, commit identity, and a closed merge decision. We implement Jankurai as a local/CI auditor and evaluate it on seeded conformance fixtures and an advisory scan of public repositories. The results show that conventional CI and project maturity do not necessarily expose merge-witness-ready evidence surfaces. Jankurai does not prove full program semantics; instead, it turns missing merge evidence into stable, repairable repository states.

This is still long, but it reads like a paper rather than a catalog.

## Introduction feedback

The first section currently opens with a sweeping language-history narrative. That material is interesting, but it delays the paper’s real contribution. A top-tier reviewer wants to know quickly:

* What is the problem?
* Why now?
* Why existing CI/CODEOWNERS/tests are insufficient?
* What is the new artifact?
* What was implemented?
* What evidence supports it?

The current first two pages spend a lot of time on assembly, C, managed runtimes, JavaScript, TypeScript, Dijkstra, Parnas, Conway, Brooks, technical debt, and language ecosystems. That is over-motivated. The running checkout PR is much stronger and should be the opening.

Recommended intro order:

1. **Paragraph 1:** AI coding creates plausible PRs faster than review systems can validate them.
2. **Paragraph 2:** Concrete checkout PR: UI + generated client + contract + tests. What makes it safe?
3. **Paragraph 3:** Existing controls are fragmented: CODEOWNERS, CI, secret scans, contract tests, screenshots, waivers. None binds everything into a single merge decision.
4. **Paragraph 4:** Jankurai’s answer: merge witness.
5. **Paragraph 5:** Contributions.
6. **Paragraph 6:** Evaluation summary.
7. **Paragraph 7:** Limitations.

Move most language-history material to Related Work or a short “Historical motivation” discussion.

## Contribution claims need to become sharper

The paper currently lists four contributions, but they read broad. I would rewrite them as measurable claims:

1. **Model:** A repository-local model that maps changed paths to ownership, generated-boundary policy, proof lanes, waivers, receipts, and repair actions.
2. **Artifact:** A schema-valid merge witness with closed decision states.
3. **Implementation:** A reference auditor that emits reports, proof plans, repair queues, and witness artifacts.
4. **Evaluation:** A conformance suite plus public-repository alignment-surface scan, with exact reproduction artifacts.

The current contribution “reports current seed conformance-suite evidence” is weak because the paper admits the current validation is inventory-only, not observed per-fixture witness decisions. That honesty is good, but for tier-1 it must be fixed before submission. The paper should not foreground a conformance suite until it can show expected-vs-observed decisions.

## Evaluation is the biggest tier-1 gap

The evaluation section is careful and honest, but it currently undercuts the paper’s ambition. It says the current seed conformance lane validates fixture inventory and expected JSON presence, not observed witness decisions. That is acceptable for a public draft, but not enough for a top-tier paper. 

You need at least one of these stronger evaluation packages:

### Evaluation package A: conformance-suite correctness

Implement the per-fixture runner and report:

* Number of fixtures.
* Expected decision.
* Observed decision.
* Primary rule.
* Required missing evidence.
* Runtime.
* Pass/fail.
* Commit SHA.
* Auditor version.
* Schema version.

The table should become:

| Fixture                      | Expected | Observed | Rule    | Result | Runtime |
| ---------------------------- | -------: | -------: | ------- | -----: | ------: |
| hl3-pass-minimal             |     pass |     pass | none    |   pass |    x ms |
| generated-zone-mutation-fail |    block |    block | HLT-002 |   pass |    x ms |
| ownerless-path-fail          |    block |    block | HLT-003 |   pass |    x ms |

Right now, “Inventory and expected JSON present” is not enough.

### Evaluation package B: injection study

Take several real repositories or project fixtures and inject controlled faults:

* Generated file edited by hand.
* Ownerless path.
* Missing proof lane.
* Secret-like fixture.
* Overbroad agent permission.
* UI change missing rendered evidence.
* Destructive migration.
* Authorization isolation gap.
* Input boundary gap.

Measure detection precision/recall on injected changes. This would strongly support the claim that vibe artifacts are detectable repository states.

### Evaluation package C: repair-locality study

Measure whether Jankurai makes agent/human repair easier:

* Baseline: agent receives repo + issue.
* Jankurai: agent receives changed paths + owner route + proof lane + repair queue.
* Metrics: first-pass repair success, proof-selection accuracy, context tokens, time to repair, number of files touched, review iterations.

This would directly support the “agent efficiency” claim.

### Evaluation package D: public scan with validation

The 30-repo advisory scan is intriguing, but it currently risks being dismissed because Jankurai scores repositories low partly for not already exposing Jankurai-specific surfaces. To make it credible:

* Record exact commit SHAs for all 30 repositories.
* Publish the full run script and Docker/container environment.
* Separate “Jankurai adoption missing” from “general engineering risk.”
* Manually validate a random sample of findings.
* Report false-positive categories.
* Avoid framing low scores as quality judgments.
* Replace “scores” with “alignment-surface prevalence” in the main paper.

The strongest version of this result is not “all these repositories score red.” It is:

> Mature repositories often have CI and tests, but rarely expose machine-readable changed-path ownership, proof routing, generated-zone receipts, and merge-witness-ready repair queues.

That is fair, defensible, and more interesting.

## Public repository scoring needs softer framing

The public scan section names prominent repositories and gives low scores. This is risky. Reviewers may read it as accusatory, even though the text says it is advisory. The table also makes the paper look like a leaderboard, which distracts from the standard.

Recommended changes:

| Generated-zone policy      |              x/30 | ...                                                              |

## The language/stack sections should mostly move out

Sections on language history, standard gravity, reference-profile scoring, stack comparison, and Rust/TypeScript/PostgreSQL architecture take too much space and change the paper’s perceived thesis. The paper says Jankurai is stack-neutral, but then spends many pages ranking or favoring stacks. That creates a contradiction, even though the text repeatedly says the profile is non-normative.

For a tier-1 paper, I would cut or move:

* “Languages as Proof-Cost Compression”
* “Technical Promise Versus Standard Gravity”
* “Non-Normative Reference Profile Score”
* “Reference Profile Comparison”
* “Reference Architecture Profile”
* “Representative Ownership Cells for the Reference Profile”

Keep at most one short discussion subsection:

> Jankurai is stack-neutral. Some stacks lower proof cost by making invalid states harder to express, but conformance is determined by emitted repository evidence, not language choice.

Everything else belongs in a separate “Jankurai Reference Profile” document.

## Rule IDs are useful, but the main paper has too many of them

Stable rule IDs are one of the paper’s best engineering ideas. But the main paper overwhelms the reader with HLT tables. You do not need all 35 rule families in the main paper.

Recommended main-paper treatment:

* Show 8-10 representative rules.
* Put the full registry in Appendix A or supplementary artifact.
* Define HLT explicitly. If HLT stands for something, say it early. If it does not, rename the prefix or explain that it is a stable rule-family namespace.
* Group rules by merge-evidence failure:

  * ownership/routing
  * generated boundary
  * proof freshness
  * security/permissions
  * UX/browser evidence
  * data/migration
  * waiver/review evidence

A top-tier reader does not need every rule on first pass. They need to believe the rule system is stable, auditable, and extensible.

## Concrete source-level layout fixes

### 1. Remove the standalone title page

The frontmatter currently builds a custom title page. For the submission version, remove it and use standard conference formatting. Keep the branded cover only for a project whitepaper.

Recommended split:

```tex
% paper/jankurai.tex
\documentclass[10pt,conference]{IEEEtran}

\input{paper/tex/preamble}
\input{paper/tex/macros}

\begin{document}

\title{Jankurai: Continuous Repository Alignment for Proof-Carrying Pull Requests}
\author{\IEEEauthorblockN{...}}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
...
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
repository alignment, proof-carrying pull requests, merge evidence, software maintenance, AI coding agents
\end{IEEEkeywords}
```

### 2. Remove most `\FloatBarrier` calls

The barriers in the wrapper are likely one of the main causes of the blank pages. Keep barriers only before appendices or major mode switches. The source currently places barriers between major sections and again before related work/conclusion, which forces LaTeX to flush floats prematurely. ([GitHub][1])

Better:

```tex
\input{paper/tex/sections/01_intro}
\input{paper/tex/sections/02_model}
\input{paper/tex/sections/03_design}
\input{paper/tex/sections/04_implementation}
\input{paper/tex/sections/05_evaluation}
\input{paper/tex/sections/06_discussion}
\input{paper/tex/sections/07_related_work}
\input{paper/tex/sections/08_conclusion}

\clearpage
\appendices
...
```

Use `\FloatBarrier` only where there is a specific reason.

### 3. Avoid one-column appendices inside the main paper

The source switches to `\onecolumn` for appendices and later returns to `\twocolumn` for references. That is a major source of page breaks and layout discontinuity. ([GitHub][1])

Fix:

* Keep appendices two-column.
* Or make a separate supplemental PDF.
* Or place references before appendices if venue permits.
* Do not switch layout modes mid-paper unless absolutely necessary.

### 4. Stop using full-width floats for text-like objects

Figure 1 and several wide tables are not visually rich enough to justify `figure*` or `table*`. Wide floats are expensive in two-column layouts.

Rule of thumb:

* If it is mostly text, make it a one-column table or prose.
* If it is a diagram, make it visually meaningful.
* If it is a registry, move it to appendix.
* If it is a schema/code example, show only the decisive excerpt.

### 5. Reduce table count in the main paper

The paper has dozens of tables. A strong main paper should probably have:

* 4-6 main tables.
* 3-5 figures.
* Full registries in appendix/supplement.

Recommended main-paper tables:

1. Running checkout example: blocked -> repaired witness.
2. Merge witness fields.
3. Representative rule families.
4. Evaluation results.
5. Public scan surface prevalence.
6. Threats/limitations summary.

Everything else moves out.

### 6. Use `flushbottom` for the submission version

The frontmatter includes `\raggedbottom`. That can make uneven bottoms and visible white gaps more acceptable to LaTeX. For an IEEE-style submission, use the default or explicitly test `\flushbottom`. ([GitHub][2])

This will not fix float-only pages by itself, but it will make columns look less ragged once the float structure is repaired.

### 7. Remove custom footer in submission mode

The custom footer makes the document look like a draft standard. It is useful for internal versioned artifacts, but a tier-1 paper should not carry “public_draft,” schema version, and paper edition in every footer. The source defines a `fancypagestyle{jankurai}` footer with those fields. ([GitHub][2])

Keep versioning in:

* artifact manifest
* reproducibility appendix
* camera-ready artifact badge
* repository release

Remove it from the main paper body.

## Writing style improvements

The prose is energetic, but it repeats the same thesis many times:

> repository cannot tell what owns the behavior, which proof applies, or what evidence would make the merge trustworthy

That line is good. Use it once in the intro and echo it once in the conclusion. Do not repeat it in every section.

Also reduce list-cadence. Many sentences enumerate 8-12 items. That makes the paper feel heavy. For example, instead of:

> owner maps, test maps, generated-zone policy, stable rule identifiers, version manifests, bounded authority, proof receipts, audit reports, repair queues, merge witnesses...

Use:

> ownership, proof routing, generated-boundary policy, receipts, waivers, and witness decisions.

Then let the model section define the full set.

## Tone adjustment for tier-1 review

Keep the term “vibe artifact,” but make the paper’s formal vocabulary sober:

* Use “unauditable change residue” or “missing merge evidence” near the definition.
* Avoid sounding like the paper is scolding developers.
* Avoid “standard war” or “stack war” energy.
* Make “vibe coding” an operational term, not a cultural insult.

The best tone is:

> We observed that AI-assisted edits expose a pre-existing weakness in repositories: evidence needed for review is scattered across tools and memory. Jankurai defines the missing binding artifact.

That is calmer and stronger.

## The non-normative profile problem

The paper repeatedly says the reference profile is non-normative, but the amount of space devoted to Rust/TypeScript/PostgreSQL makes it feel normative anyway. This is a credibility risk.

Best fix:

* Move profile scoring and stack bands out of the main paper.
* Keep only the statement: “Any stack can conform if it emits equivalent artifacts.”
* If you want a profile, publish it as `Jankurai Reference Profile: Rust/TypeScript/PostgreSQL`.

That separation will make the core standard much more defensible.

## Source organization feedback

The source is modular by section, which is good: the `paper/tex/sections` directory contains separate TeX files for bottleneck, definitions/threat model, language compression, standard gravity, taxonomy, standard, evaluation, stack rubric, stack ranking, architecture, agent controls, continuous proof, pixel QA, security, repair, governance, related work, and conclusion. ([GitHub][3])

But the frontmatter is too compressed and overloaded. It currently combines document class, packages, table colors, list settings, code style, footer style, hyperref metadata, header graphic, metadata block, abstract block, title page, and document start. Split it into:

* `paper/tex/preamble.tex`
* `paper/tex/macros/tables.tex`
* `paper/tex/macros/code.tex`
* `paper/tex/frontmatter_submission.tex`
* `paper/tex/frontmatter_techreport.tex`

Then make two build targets:

* `just paper-submission`
* `just paper-techreport`

Submission build: no logo cover, no custom footer, fewer appendices.

Tech-report build: branded cover, full standard appendix, version footer.

## Recommended main-paper artifact budget

For a polished submission:

* **Pages:** 10-12 main pages, plus references.
* **Figures:** 4.
* **Tables:** 5.
* **Appendices:** separate supplement.
* **Code blocks:** 1 small witness excerpt only.
* **Terms:** 6 core terms.
* **Rules in main:** 8-10 representative HLT rules.
* **Evaluation:** at least one observed result table.

## The single highest-impact content rewrite

Rewrite the paper around this claim:

> Jankurai turns review uncertainty into a versioned merge witness.

Then every section should answer one question:

* What uncertainty exists?
* What repository artifact represents it?
* How does the auditor detect it?
* What evidence satisfies it?
* How does the witness decide?
* What repair action follows?

That framing will make the paper much cleaner.

## The single highest-impact layout fix

Remove the standalone title page, remove most `\FloatBarrier` calls, and move the one-column appendices into a separate supplement. Those three changes alone will eliminate much of the worst white area. The source currently has exactly the constructs that cause the clunky pages: repeated barriers, forced clear pages, and mid-document column switching. ([GitHub][1])

## Final priority list

**Priority 1: Focus**
Make this a merge-witness paper, not a full standard manual.

**Priority 2: Evaluation**
Implement observed conformance fixture results and add a validation study.

**Priority 3: Layout**
Remove titlepage, barriers, one-column appendix, and float-only tables.

**Priority 4: Cut**
Move stack/profile/ranking/CLI/template material to supplement.

**Priority 5: Rebuild visuals**
One strong architecture figure, one worked example, one results chart.

**Priority 6: Tone**
Lead with “repository-local merge evidence,” not “anti-vibe” rhetoric.

The paper has a compelling center. The best version will feel much more disciplined: fewer tables, more observed evidence, one clear artifact, one concrete running example, and a layout that lets the argument breathe without wasting pages.
