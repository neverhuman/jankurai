I read the 37-page PDF as a paper, as a prospective standard, and as an adoption artifact. The core idea is genuinely important: **Jankurai’s strongest contribution is the move from “AI coding policy” to “auditable merge evidence.”** The manuscript already frames the scarce act as “trustworthy merge” and defines the public standard around conformance artifacts, proof lanes, evidence indexes, repair queues, merge witnesses, and versioned governance. That is the right center of gravity.

My top-level recommendation: **make the paper less like a manifesto plus stack ranking, and more like the birth document for a durable standard.** The shocking part should not be that it says “Humans Were the Bug.” The shocking part should be that a repository can emit a machine-readable answer to: **“Is this merge allowed, what evidence proves it, what is missing, who owns the risk, and what expires?”**

## Executive diagnosis

The paper has the seed of a tier-1 idea. It is trying to define an auditable repository control plane for AI-era software work. The best phrasing is already present: **“no proof, no merge; no receipt, no trust.”** The merge witness, proof receipt, generated-zone policy, owner map, test map, conformance levels, and repair queue are the pieces that could become widely adopted.

The main risk is that the paper currently tries to be four documents at once:

1. A public manifesto against vibe coding.
2. A formal repository standard.
3. A reference architecture and stack recommendation.
4. A generated implementation manual with command examples and long coverage tables.

For wide acceptance, split those layers clearly. The paper should become the persuasive, citable design argument. The standard should become a separate normative specification. The reference profile should be explicitly optional. The long rule registry and coverage table should become generated companion evidence, not the visual bulk of the paper.

The second major risk is **credibility balance**. The paper’s strongest claims are careful and bounded, especially where it says Jankurai does not prove all program semantics and does not have the institutional standing of IETF, W3C, CNCF, SPDX, OpenAPI, or OpenSSF. Keep that humility. It makes the work more trustworthy. But the tone sometimes shifts into language-war or slogan territory. For tier-1 reception, the standard must feel inevitable, not combative.

The third major risk is **lack of empirical evaluation**. The paper has a strong design, citations, and taxonomy, but to gain serious academic/professional acceptance it needs a reproducibility bundle: example repositories, seeded failures, expected audit output, baseline comparisons, false-positive rates, proof-lane timing, repair-locality metrics, and case studies.

The fourth major risk is **layout overload**. Pages 18-30 are dominated by extremely dense colored source-row coverage tables. That material is valuable as evidence, but not as paper body. It should become an HTML registry, CSV/JSON artifact, or appendix excerpt. The paper needs cleaner figures: the merge-witness schema, the conformance ladder, the proof loop, and the adoption path.

## What is already excellent

The central concept is unusually strong. “Trustworthy merge” is the right bottleneck. The paper correctly recognizes that AI changes the cost of producing plausible code but does not automatically change the cost of proving correctness, security, ownership, or maintainability.

The standard/profile separation is also excellent. The abstract already says the standard is distinct from the reference profile: Rust core, TypeScript/React/Vite, PostgreSQL, generated contracts, and bounded Python. That distinction is critical because adoption will fail if readers believe Jankurai equals “you must use my favorite stack.”

The conformance levels HL0-HL5 are a strong adoption mechanism. Progressive adoption is essential. A standard that only supports “all in” adoption will not survive brownfield reality.

The merge witness is probably the most important primitive in the whole paper. It turns the slogan into something auditable:

> merge = changed paths + owner route + proof receipts + score delta + missing evidence decision

That should be visually central, probably in the first two pages.

The repair queue and repair receipts are also major. Most AI-coding discussions stop at prevention. Jankurai’s deeper insight is that **repair evidence becomes reusable repository memory**.

The rendered UX section is surprisingly important. Treating the browser as a runtime that requires proof, not just screenshots, gives the paper practical reach beyond backend/security audiences.

The versioned artifact manifest is exactly the kind of mechanism that makes this more than prose. The standard should lean harder into that.

## Highest-impact changes before publication

### 1. Rename the paper’s public promise around “trustworthy merge”

Current title stack:

> Jankurai
> A Versioned Agent-Native Repository Standard for Trustworthy Merge
> Humans Were the Bug: From Vibe Coding to Agent-Native Engineering

Recommended title stack:

> **Jankurai: A Versioned Agent-Native Repository Standard for Trustworthy Merge**
> **No Proof, No Merge; No Receipt, No Trust**

Use “Humans Were the Bug” as a blog headline, section epigraph, or conference-talk subtitle, not the main academic subtitle. It is memorable, but it can alienate exactly the senior engineers, security reviewers, standards people, and maintainers whose buy-in you need. A more precise version is:

> **Human-memory-based repositories were the bug.**

That preserves the point while avoiding the implication that people themselves are the defect.

### 2. Move the standard before the stack argument

The current paper spends significant early energy on programming language history, standard gravity, and stack ranking. Those sections are interesting, but they delay the main contribution. Readers should encounter the standard mechanics earlier.

Suggested order:

1. Problem: trustworthy merge is the bottleneck.
2. Threat model and definitions.
3. Jankurai standard: artifacts, conformance levels, proof lanes, rule IDs.
4. Merge witness and proof receipt schema.
5. Continuous audit loop.
6. Evaluation and case studies.
7. Reference profile: Rust/TypeScript/PostgreSQL/bounded Python.
8. Migration and governance.
9. Limitations.

This makes the paper less vulnerable to being dismissed as a Rust/stack manifesto.

### 3. Add a real evaluation section

For tier-1 acceptance, the paper needs original evidence. Add a section called **Evaluation** or **Implementation Evidence**. It should include at least three repositories or benchmark fixtures:

A minimal demo repo with seeded faults: generated-zone mutation, missing proof lane, secret leak, handwritten DTO drift, unsafe migration, missing rendered UX evidence.

A brownfield repo adoption case: start at HL0/HL1, run `jankurai adopt`, show findings, add owner/test maps, reach HL2/HL3.

A reference-profile repo: Rust + TS/Vite + Postgres + bounded Python, showing merge witness output on real PRs.

Useful metrics:

| Metric                     | Why it matters                                               |
| -------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Route accuracy             | Did changed paths map to the correct owners and proof lanes? |
| Missing-evidence detection | Did Jankurai catch proof gaps ordinary CI missed?            |
| Repair locality            | How many files did an agent need to inspect or edit?         |
| False positives            | Which findings were noisy or non-actionable?                 |
| False negatives            | Which seeded failures escaped detection?                     |
| CI cost                    | Does conformance remain practical?                           |
| Token reduction            | Does context-pack reduce irrelevant context?                 |
| Review time                | Does the merge witness make review faster?                   |
| Regression prevention      | Does ratchet mode stop posture decay?                        |

Without this, the paper is a powerful design proposal. With it, it becomes a serious standard candidate.

### 4. Split the deliverables

The “tier-1” version should ship as a small ecosystem:

| Artifact                    | Purpose                                                                      |
| --------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| `paper/jankurai.pdf`        | Citable argument and design rationale.                                       |
| `spec/jankurai-standard.md` | Normative standard using MUST/SHOULD/MAY.                                    |
| `schemas/*.json`            | Machine-readable report, witness, proof receipt, rule, and manifest schemas. |
| `conformance/`              | Fixtures and expected outputs.                                               |
| `registry/rules.html`       | Human-readable rule registry.                                                |
| `examples/`                 | Reference repos and before/after PRs.                                        |
| `governance/`               | Versioning, deprecation, extension, and proposal process.                    |

This mirrors what has worked elsewhere: BCP 14 gives precise normative language for MUST/SHOULD/MAY; SemVer gives version meaning; SPDX publishes current and previous specification versions; OpenAPI separates specification text from schemas and tooling; W3C uses maturity stages; CNCF conformance relies on submitted runnable test evidence; OpenSSF Scorecard succeeds partly because it turns posture into automated, consumable checks. ([RFC Editor][1])

### 5. Make the merge witness the hero artifact

A reader should see a merge witness example by page 3. Show a compact JSON object and its Markdown summary:

```json
{
  "standard_version": "0.6.1",
  "schema_version": "1.4.1",
  "level_claim": "HL3",
  "base": "origin/main",
  "head": "feature/payment-copy",
  "changed_paths": [
    "apps/web/src/checkout/PaymentForm.tsx",
    "contracts/openapi/api.yaml"
  ],
  "required_lanes": ["web", "contract", "security"],
  "receipts": [
    {"lane": "contract", "status": "pass", "artifact": "target/jankurai/contract.json"},
    {"lane": "web", "status": "missing", "required_artifact": "playwright-trace.zip"}
  ],
  "decision": "block",
  "blocking_findings": [
    {
      "rule_id": "HLT-013-RENDERED-UX-GAP",
      "owner": "apps/web",
      "repair": "run mapped Playwright proof and attach screenshot/trace evidence"
    }
  ]
}
```

Then show the reviewer-facing summary:

> **Decision:** Block
> **Reason:** UI changed without rendered browser evidence.
> **Owner:** apps/web
> **Required lane:** web
> **Repair:** run `jankurai prove --changed apps/web/src/checkout/PaymentForm.tsx`

That is where people will understand the power.

# Section-by-section feedback

## Title page

**What works:**
The title “A Versioned Agent-Native Repository Standard for Trustworthy Merge” is strong, specific, and memorable. The version metadata on the title page immediately signals that this is not just a thought piece. The logo makes the project feel real.

**What to improve:**
The title page currently feels more like a branded product launch than a standards paper. For an academic or standards audience, reduce the logo size or move the large mascot/wordmark to a project whitepaper version. Keep the version metadata, but place it in a small footnote or compact metadata block.

The subtitle “Humans Were the Bug” is attention-grabbing, but it risks creating resistance. The paper’s own nuance is better: humans were not the bug because they wrote code; repositories organized around human memory were the bug. That should be the thesis line.

**Suggested title-page layout:**

> Jankurai: A Versioned Agent-Native Repository Standard for Trustworthy Merge
> No Proof, No Merge; No Receipt, No Trust
> standard_version=0.6.1 · schema_version=1.4.1 · paper_edition=2026.05-ed6
> Jankurai Project

Keep the logo, but make it smaller and cleaner.

## Abstract

**What works:**
The abstract does a good job naming the bottleneck: not code generation, but trustworthy merge. It clearly states three contributions: the repository standard, the conformance/audit mechanism, and the separation between standard and reference profile.

**What to improve:**
It is currently dense with lists. That is appropriate for a spec, but abstracts need a sharper narrative. The reader should leave with one sentence burned in:

> Jankurai makes merge decisions reproducible by requiring changed paths, ownership, proof lanes, receipts, and missing-evidence decisions to be emitted as versioned artifacts.

Also, the abstract should mention evaluation if added. If there is no evaluation yet, avoid language that sounds like empirical validation.

**Possible revised abstract direction:**

> AI-assisted development lowers the cost of producing plausible code but raises the importance of proving whether a change is safe to merge. This paper introduces Jankurai, a versioned repository standard for making merge decisions reproducible across human- and agent-authored changes. Jankurai defines machine-readable ownership maps, proof-lane routing, generated-zone policy, version manifests, stable rule identifiers, proof receipts, repair queues, and merge witnesses. A repository claiming conformance must emit current audit artifacts that bind changed paths to owners, required proof, observed evidence, missing evidence, exceptions, and merge decisions.
>
> The standard is independent of its reference profile. The reference profile uses Rust for durable core behavior, TypeScript/React/Vite for product UI, PostgreSQL for durable truth, generated contracts for public boundaries, and bounded Python for AI/data-service work. Jankurai does not prove full program semantics; it defines a practical, auditable control plane for rejecting unproven changes, routing repair, and versioning repository posture over time.

## Index terms

**What works:**
The current index terms are reasonable.

**Improve:**
Add terms that better match the novel contribution:

> AI-assisted software engineering, agentic coding, repository conformance, software supply chain, continuous audit, proof receipts, merge gates, generated code governance, CI/CD, repair automation.

## I. New Bottleneck: Trustworthy Merge

**What works:**
This is one of the strongest sections. The paper’s public thesis is memorable, and the loop is excellent:

> intent → bounded authority → proof lanes → evidence → repair or exception expiry → reusable primitives

That loop should become a figure. It is more important than some of the later charts.

**What to improve:**
Start with a concrete PR story. For example:

> An agent changes a checkout component and a generated API client. Traditional review sees a plausible diff. Jankurai sees two changed ownership cells, one generated-zone touch, one missing contract regeneration receipt, and no rendered UX proof. The merge witness blocks the PR and emits a repair queue.

That would make the standard feel immediately practical.

The four claims at the end of the section are good, but the stack claim should be softened or moved. Claim 4 currently shifts from standard to stack too early. Replace it with:

> Fourth, a reference profile demonstrates how the standard can be instantiated, but the conformance claim belongs to the repository evidence, not the stack.

**Layout:**
The arrow loop should be rendered as a horizontal figure, not monospace text. Add a figure caption: “The Jankurai merge-control loop.”

## II. Definitions and Threat Model

**What works:**
Operational definitions are exactly right. “Conformance is a public interface, not a vibe” is one of the best lines in the paper. The distinction between continuous audit and a hosted daemon is also important.

**What to improve:**
This section needs a more formal threat-model shape. Add:

| Threat-model element | Needed content                                                                                                                                     |
| -------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Assets               | Source code, secrets, credentials, schemas, generated artifacts, audit evidence, production data, CI permissions.                                  |
| Actors               | Human developer, coding agent, reviewer, malicious contributor, compromised dependency, poisoned tool/MCP server, untrusted issue/web/doc content. |
| Trust boundaries     | Trusted policy, repo code, generated artifacts, proof evidence, untrusted input, external package registry, CI runner, browser runtime.            |
| In scope             | False plausibility, prompt injection, tool overreach, secret leakage, generated-zone mutation, false-green tests, unsafe migrations.               |
| Out of scope         | Full semantic correctness, formal proof of all behavior, legal compliance certification, proof of malicious maintainer honesty.                    |
| Assumptions          | Repository has VCS history, CI or local proof runner, stable artifact paths, and a declared conformance target.                                    |

Also, define “current” precisely. A conformant artifact is current if it was generated against the candidate commit, includes the repository revision, and its artifact digests match the working tree or CI checkout.

The word “ledger” is powerful, but if the proof ledger is not cryptographically append-only, define it carefully as an appendable evidence set, not as blockchain-like immutability.

## III. Languages as Bottleneck Compression

**What works:**
The “languages as bottleneck-compression systems” idea is genuinely good. It gives a historical lens without just saying “Rust good, Python bad.”

**What to improve:**
This section is interesting but currently competes with the main standard. Shorten it. Its purpose should be to explain why repository-level proof matters more than syntax familiarity in the agent era.

Avoid making broad claims that could trigger language-community defensiveness. The point is not that one language is morally better. The point is that languages and repo structures encode different proof costs.

Suggested framing:

> In agent-native engineering, language choice matters insofar as it changes rejection speed, proof locality, boundary clarity, and repair evidence.

Move the long historical table to an appendix or condense it to five rows.

## IV. Technical Promise Versus Standard Gravity

**What works:**
This section correctly distinguishes technical elegance from default-standard adoption. That is an important point, especially when discussing Julia, Elm, Haskell, Scala, Elixir, and other high-quality ecosystems.

**What to improve:**
The section may feel like a language ranking detour. In a standards paper, it should answer: **why do standards need boring adoption mechanics?** Use this section to motivate conformance suites, examples, migration paths, and compatibility, not to evaluate languages.

Rename it:

> **Why Technical Merit Is Not Enough for Agent-Native Adoption**

Then tie it directly to Jankurai:

> Jankurai should not win because it has a clever philosophy. It should win only if its artifacts are easy to install, test, compare, and update.

## V. Vibe-Coding Fault Taxonomy

**What works:**
This is one of the paper’s major assets. Turning “vibe coding” into rule IDs, lanes, evidence requirements, and repair receipts is exactly how the concept becomes auditable.

The Top-Level Risk breakdown and RPN model are useful. The paper is careful to say RPN is a Jankurai policy weight, not a universal incident statistic. Keep that caveat.

**What to improve:**
The taxonomy needs calibration and cleanup before public release.

First, explain the RPN method in one paragraph or a small table:

| Score dimension      | 1               | 3                     | 5                                         |
| -------------------- | --------------- | --------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| Impact               | Local annoyance | User-visible defect   | Security/data/business loss               |
| Likelihood           | Rare            | Plausible             | Common in AI-assisted workflows           |
| Detection difficulty | Obvious         | Requires mapped proof | Often invisible without targeted evidence |

Second, rename “absolute coverage” to something more precise. “Absolute” sounds too strong. Use:

* `implemented`
* `partial`
* `not_covered`
* `not_applicable`

Third, audit the rule mappings. Some appendix rows appear questionable. For example, XSS/unsafe rendering should probably map to input/output boundary or rendered UX/a11y rules, not dependency supply-chain. Broken business logic should not map to supply-chain drift. Prompt-level rules treated as security controls may belong under agent-tool supply or prompt-injection governance, not authorization isolation. These mappings are fixable, but they matter because the paper’s credibility depends on the rule registry feeling exact.

Fourth, move the enormous source-row coverage table out of the main PDF. Keep a one-page summary in the paper and publish the full generated table as HTML/CSV/JSON.

**Layout:**
Table IV is dense but acceptable. Appendix Table XXIII across pages 18-30 is not. It overwhelms the reader and is color-dependent. It should become a generated registry with filters by TLR, rule, coverage state, and evidence kind.

## VI. Jankurai Standard and Conformance

**What works:**
This is the paper’s core. The standard mechanics are exactly where Jankurai becomes important: conformance levels, stable rule IDs, proof lanes, generated zones, owner maps, test maps, audit outputs, proof receipts, repair queues, ratchets, and release gates.

The adoption ladder is excellent:

> agents → score → ci → full → ratchet

That is realistic and adoption-friendly.

**What to improve:**
Move this section earlier. It should appear before the language and stack discussion.

Make the normative core more formal. Create numbered requirements:

| Requirement ID | Requirement                                                                                                             |
| -------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| JKR-001        | A conformant repository MUST declare `standard_version`, `schema_version`, and `target_stack_id`.                       |
| JKR-002        | A conformant repository MUST provide an owner map covering changed paths.                                               |
| JKR-003        | A conformant repository MUST provide proof-lane routing for changed paths.                                              |
| JKR-004        | A conformant repository MUST identify generated zones and reject hand edits unless source regeneration evidence exists. |
| JKR-005        | A conformant repository MUST emit a merge witness for PR/merge decisions.                                               |

Use BCP 14 terms only in the normative spec, and keep them uppercase when they have normative force. BCP 14 specifically clarifies requirement keywords such as MUST, SHOULD, and MAY, with RFC 8174 clarifying uppercase usage. ([RFC Editor][1])

Add explicit conformance claim examples:

```toml
[jankurai]
standard_version = "0.6.1"
schema_version = "1.4.1"
level = "HL3"
target_profile = "rust-ts-vite-react-postgres-bounded-python"
claim_scope = "repository"
```

Also add a conformance test kit:

```bash
jankurai conformance run . \
  --level HL3 \
  --out target/jankurai/conformance.json
```

For wide adoption, people need to know exactly how to prove a claim.

**What works:**
This is another core section. It explains the local/PR/CI proof loop concretely. The path from changed files to owner map, test map, proof plan, command receipts, evidence index, audit, witness, and repair queue is the actual product.

**What to improve:**

## XIV. Exceptions, Observability, and Repair Receipts

**What works:**
This is one of the best conceptual sections. The idea that exceptions should teach agents what invariant failed and how to repair it is excellent.

**Important terminology issue:**
The paper uses “exceptions” in two senses:

1. Temporary standard waivers.
2. Runtime/program errors.

That can confuse readers. Rename the first to **waivers** or **deviations**:

* `docs/waivers/<ID>.md`
* `jankurai waivers expire`
* “Temporary waivers are allowed only when scoped, owned, testable, and expiring.”

Reserve “exceptions” for runtime errors.

**What to improve:**
Add lifecycle states:

```text
proposed → accepted → active → expiring → expired → removed
```

Add redaction rules. Repair receipts must not leak secrets, PII, privileged paths, or proprietary incident details.

Add a “safe public receipt” vs “private internal receipt” distinction.

## XV. Migration, Versioning, and Governance

**What works:**
The migration sequence is realistic. “No-write adoption” is an excellent principle. Brownfield teams need observe mode, advisory scoring, and ratchets only after baseline acceptance.

The versioned artifact manifest is central and should be elevated.

**What to improve:**
Move much of this earlier, because governance is part of why a standard is credible.

Add a Jankurai governance model:

| Governance element | Recommendation                                                              |
| ------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Proposal process   | JEP: Jankurai Enhancement Proposal.                                         |
| Version policy     | SemVer for schemas and auditor behavior; separate paper editions.           |
| Rule lifecycle     | draft → candidate → stable → superseded → obsolete.                         |
| Compatibility      | Breaking schema/report changes require major version.                       |
| Deprecation        | Every superseded rule gets migration notes.                                 |
| Conformance suite  | Required before rules become stable.                                        |
| Registry           | Public rule registry with current/previous versions.                        |
| Extension model    | Vendor/project-specific rules under namespaced IDs.                         |
| Badge policy       | Badge must state level, standard version, auditor version, date, and scope. |

This is the part that can make Jankurai feel like a serious standard rather than a single-repo tool.

## XVI. Limitations and Research Agenda

**What works:**
The limitations section is appropriately humble. It acknowledges that audit cannot prove every semantic property, that weights need calibration, and that alternate stacks can conform.

**What to improve:**
Make the research agenda sharper and more testable.

Add these limitations:

* Proof lanes can be wrong, stale, too slow, or too narrow.
* Owner maps can encode organizational politics or outdated ownership.
* Generated-zone policy does not guarantee source contract correctness.
* Security scanners have false negatives.
* Rendered UX checks may create false positives.
* Audit artifacts can leak sensitive information unless redacted.
* Conformance can become ceremony if not tied to escaped-defect and repair metrics.
* A malicious maintainer can still approve bad policy unless governance protects changes to the control plane.

Add research questions:

| Question                                           | Possible study                                                       |
| -------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Does Jankurai reduce escaped defects?              | Seeded-fault benchmark across repositories.                          |
| Does it improve agent repair locality?             | Compare context size and files touched with/without owner/test maps. |
| Does ratchet mode prevent decay?                   | Longitudinal repo study.                                             |
| Which HLT rules are noisy?                         | False-positive/false-negative reporting.                             |
| Which proof lanes create most value per CI minute? | Cost/benefit analysis.                                               |

## XVII. Conclusion

**What works:**
The conclusion is strong and memorable. It returns to the thesis that teams optimizing for human comfort alone will get faster at creating ambiguity, while verification-oriented teams will get faster at rejecting and repairing bad code.

**What to improve:**
End on the standard, not the stack. The current conclusion says the default target is Rust core, TypeScript/React/Vite, PostgreSQL, generated contracts, and bounded Python. That is fine, but the final sentence should be about auditable merge evidence.

Suggested ending:

> The repository must become a verification interface. In an agent-native repository, a merge is not trusted because the patch looks plausible or because the author sounds confident. It is trusted because changed paths route to owners, proof lanes produce receipts, missing evidence is explicit, waivers expire, and repair becomes reusable evidence. No proof, no merge; no receipt, no trust.

# The most important message to preserve

The paper should not merely argue that AI coding is risky. Many people are already saying that. Its unique message is stronger:

> **The repository must become the interface through which agents and humans prove merge readiness.**

That is the profound standard-level idea. The mature version of Jankurai should let a repo say:

> This change touched these paths.
> These owners are responsible.
> These lanes were required.
> These commands ran.
> These artifacts prove it.
> These findings remain.
> These waivers expire.
> This merge is allowed, blocked, or accepted under ratchet.

That is the auditable standard worth building around.
